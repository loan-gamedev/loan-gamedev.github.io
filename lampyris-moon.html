Lampyris Moon

A narrative psychological horror game where a strange night unfolds a disturbing story through the eyes of the one who lived it.

Overview

On a night when the moon seems different, a lone individual ventures into a silent but oppressive environment. Very quickly, a strange feeling takes hold — as if something is watching, lurking in the shadows.

Lampyris Moon is a first-person narrative psychological horror game that I designed, developed, and published entirely on my own. The goal was simple and hard at the same time: make the player feel something through mechanics, not just cutscenes. Build tension through systems. Let the world do the storytelling.

The game was released on Steam on May 10, 2026, under my studio name Lawnite.

Key Features

NPC Dialogue System

Mysterious characters who know more than they let on. Conversations that can guide — or mislead — the player through the narrative.

Environmental Puzzles

Logic that seems to have shifted. Players must observe carefully and interact with a world that reacts unpredictably to their presence.

Multiple Enemy AI

Different entities haunt the world, each with its own behaviour pattern and intentions. State-machine-driven enemy logic implemented from scratch.

Narrative Horror Design

The story is experienced through gameplay, not told through cutscenes. Tension and dread are built into the systems themselves.

Technical Challenges

Narrative Through Mechanics

The hardest problem was making gameplay carry the narrative without relying solely on dialogue or scripted events. Every system had to serve both a functional and an emotional purpose — the way an enemy behaves, how the environment responds, what objects are interactable.

Enemy Behaviour Architecture

Building multiple enemy types with distinct, believable behaviours required a clean state machine architecture in C#. Each entity needed to feel different — not just look different — so I designed their movement, detection, and reaction systems independently with shared base logic.

Solo Full-Cycle Development

Working alone from concept to Steam release meant handling everything: gameplay programming, level design, dialogue writing, asset integration, store page setup, and bug fixing under real-world conditions.

What I Learned

01

Gameplay is a narrative tool

Making a player feel fear through a mechanic — not a jumpscare — is genuinely difficult. This project taught me to think of every system as a storytelling decision.

02

Shipping is different from building

There's a huge gap between a project that works and a product that's ready. Going through the Steam publishing process taught me what shipping actually means.

03

Constraints force better decisions

Working alone with limited time forced me to cut scope, prioritize, and focus on what actually makes the experience. That discipline carried into every project after.
