Inspired by Gravity Rush

Gravity Rush (PlayStation) features a unique mechanic where the player can redefine the direction of gravity at will — shifting their weight toward any surface, flying through the air, and performing dive attacks. Reproducing this system from scratch was the core challenge of this project.

This prototype is a Blueprint-only reproduction of Gravity Rush's two signature mechanics: the gravity shift system and the aerial attack. The goal was to reverse-engineer the feel of an existing mechanic and rebuild it from first principles inside Unreal Engine 5.

No external plugins. No C++. Pure Blueprint logic — which meant understanding not just what the mechanic does, but why it feels the way it does.

Key Features

Gravity Shift System

The player can redirect gravity in real time toward any direction. The character's orientation, physics, and camera all adapt dynamically to the new gravity axis.

Aerial Attack Mechanic

While in freefall or gravity-shifted state, the player can launch a directed dive attack — locking onto a target direction and propelling toward it at high speed.

Dynamic Character Orientation

The character mesh and movement direction rotate smoothly to match the current gravity direction, preserving momentum across transitions.

Blueprint Architecture

The entire system is built in Blueprint with clean separation between gravity logic, movement, camera, and attack — each in its own component for readability.

Technical Challenges

Reproducing the "Feel" of Gravity Rush

The hardest part wasn't the physics math — it was getting the transitions to feel right. Gravity Rush's shifts feel fluid and intentional, not jarring. Achieving that in Blueprint required careful tuning of interpolation curves, camera lag, and momentum preservation across gravity changes.

Custom Gravity in Unreal Engine

UE5's default character movement component assumes a fixed downward gravity. Overriding this in Blueprint — redirecting gravity, modifying the movement plane, and keeping the character grounded on arbitrary surfaces — required a deep dive into how the engine handles physics and movement at the component level.

Camera Behaviour During Gravity Shift

The camera needed to follow the gravity shift smoothly without disorienting the player. Getting the rotation order and interpolation right so the player always has a clear sense of "up" — even mid-shift — was one of the trickiest parts of the project.
